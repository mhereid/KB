# Tactical Barbell Presents: Ageless Athlete (Book Notes)

**Source:** Tactical Barbell Presents: Ageless Athlete by Jim Madden (2017, ISBN 9781544080826)
**Captured:** 2026-04-08

---

## Overview

Ageless Athlete is the longevity-focused book in the Tactical Barbell system, written by Jim Madden (philosophy professor, former NCAA D-III football player, former competitive powerlifter). The book applies TB principles to lifelong fitness for athletes in their 30s, 40s, 50s, and beyond. Central thesis: a multidimensional approach to fitness (strength + conditioning + endurance + skills) is both more effective and more sustainable than single-domain specialization, and the key adaptations for aging athletes are recovery optimization, moderation, and discipline over emotional intensity.

## Author Background

Jim Madden transformed from a 300+ lb former powerlifter/football player at age 30 to a 188 lb multidimensional athlete by age 40. At 40 he ran a 21-min 5K trail race, deadlifted 500x3, ran a 5:56 mile, did 20 dead-hang pull-ups, and later bench pressed 350, squatted 500, deadlifted 600 while in the 190s. Has since competed in half-marathons, triathlons, and BJJ. Credits kettlebells (Pavel Tsatsouline's programs) as the catalyst for his shift from pure strength to multidimensional training. Now structures all training around Tactical Barbell.

## Part 1: Psychological Framework

### Law of Dominant Thought
Actions follow thoughts and mental images. Focus on what you want to achieve, not what you want to avoid. Defining your training by limitations ("Since I'm forty, I can't...") is a self-fulfilling prophecy of failure. Instead, define goals in positive terms ("I'm going to run a sub-21 min 5K").

### Self-Serving Bias & Fundamental Attribution Error
We attribute others' success to their innate gifts or cheating, while attributing our own failures to external circumstances. This distorts our self-assessment and creates excuses. Very fit people aren't miracles — they've exploited the same laws of physics available to everyone through years of consistent training and nutrition.

### Patience as Core Virtue
Think in terms of years and decades, not weeks and months. Madden's transformation took a full decade (age 30 to 40). Stop distorting thinking with "12 weeks to elite fitness" promises. Impatience leads to discouragement, injury, and frustration.

## Part 2: Why Train Like an Operational Athlete

### Achieving Balance Through Moderation
Aristotle's golden mean applied to fitness: the ideal is between doing too little and too much. The ageless athlete practices moderation in all training decisions — volume, intensity, frequency, exercise selection.

### Three Key Challenges for Aging Athletes
1. **Greater recovery needs** — can't bounce back as quickly between sessions
2. **Overuse/nagging injuries** — accumulated wear requires working around problems
3. **Need for small muscle gains with minimal weight gain** — maintaining functional mass without bulk

### TB Principles That Address These Challenges
- **Lowest Effective Dose** — do only what's necessary to progress; optimize recovery
- **Multi-phase programming** — rotate emphasis between attributes to manage overuse
- **Golden Rule preserved** — minimum 2-min rest between sets, even when adding volume for hypertrophy; extend to 4-5 min to avoid lactic acid buildup

## Part 3: Strength Programming

### How Strong Do You Need To Be?
Strength is a necessary but not sufficient condition for most goals. Don't confuse necessary with sufficient — unless training for a powerlifting meet, strength is just one attribute among several. Ask: "How much difference would 25 lbs on my squat make for my actual goals?" Balance strength against other needed attributes. Once you exceed strength thresholds for your goals, don't sacrifice other domains for marginal strength gains.

### Strength Templates for Ageless Athletes
Uses standard TB1 templates (Operator and Zulu) with modifications:
- **Operator** recommended as primary template (3 days/week, high frequency)
- **Zulu** for those wanting 4-day split or more exercise variety
- **Fighter** for heavy conditioning/skills loads
- Same percentage progressions as TB1 (70→80→90→75→85→95% for Operator Standard)

### Recommended Cluster
Default: Back Squat / Bench Press / Weighted Pull-up, with occasional deadlifting (1x/week or every 2nd week), and kettlebell swings as frequent accessory. This covers squat, press, pulling, and hip hinge patterns.

### Deadlift Management for Aging Athletes
Deadlift is high-cost/high-risk for many aging lifters. Madden's approach: 1-2 deadlift sessions per 6-week block, pulling 1-2 triples at 80-85%. Treat deadlift like endurance work in a Black protocol — sprinkle in as needed. Always perform on Day 5 (last lifting day of the week) in place of WPU.

### Exercise Variations for Injuries

**Shoulder difficulties:**
- Floor press, narrow grip bench press, football bar pressing
- Safety bar squat
- Overhead pressing with dumbbells or kettlebells
- Varied pull-up hand positions

**Back difficulties:**
- Elevated deadlifts (shorter ROM), Romanian deadlifts (strict form)
- Trap bar deadlifts, axle deadlifts
- Front squats

**Knee difficulties:**
- Pushing/pulling a sled (replace squatting)
- Front squat (lower load), high box squat (no rebound)
- Trap bar deadlifts

General rule: treat variations as corrective measures, not permanent replacements. Work back to full ROM versions when possible. The Big Four (Squat/Bench/Deadlift/OHP) remain the ideal.

### Accessory Exercises
Only three recommended accessories — all emphasize posterior chain, core, grip:

1. **Kettlebell Swing** — the mainstay; constant since 2006
2. **Plank and Shank** — standard plank followed by static hold back extension; use weighted vest; one all-out round
3. **Dumbbell Farmer Carry** — 2 carries of ~100 yards; use weight that puts you near grip failure; 2+ min rest between sets

### KB Swing Protocol 1: Basic Swings Periodization

Start with a KB you can swing for 100 reps (sets of 10L-10R 1H or 20x 2H), following the Golden Rule. Volume tracks the intensity week of Operator:

**Operator schedule:**

| Week | Day 1 | Day 3 | Day 5 |
|------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 (70%) | 70 swings (10L-10R x3 + 5L-5R x1) | Farmer Carry | 70 swings |
| 2 (80%) | 80 swings (10L-10R x4) | Plank & Shank | 80 swings |
| 3 (90%) | 90 swings (10L-10R x4 + 5L-5R x1) | Farmer Carry | 90 swings |

**Zulu schedule:**

| Week | Day 1 | Day 2 | Day 4 | Day 5 |
|------|-------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 (70%) | SQ/BP/WPU + 70 swings | OHP/Row + Farmer Carry | SQ/BP/WPU | OHP/DL + 70 swings |
| 2 (80%) | SQ/BP/WPU + 80 swings | OHP/Row + Plank & Shank | SQ/BP/WPU | OHP/DL + 80 swings |
| 3 (90%) | SQ/BP/WPU + 90 swings | OHP/Row + Farmer Carry | SQ/BP/WPU | OHP/DL + 90 swings |

Use 1H swings on strength days (more rest = safer technique), 2H swings during conditioning. Round down percentages (use 70-80-90 even with I/A percentages). If doing KB conditioning (Apex, BOO, etc.) on Day 6, skip swings on Day 5. If deadlifting, keep DL to 1x/week and reduce or skip swings on DL day.

### KB Swing Protocol 2: 100 Swing Challenge

Goal: 100 continuous swings with a challenging KB without putting it down. Start with max continuous set, rest, repeat to 100 total. Progressively combine sets over weeks.

**Operator schedule:**

| Week | Day 1 | Day 3 | Day 5 |
|------|-------|-------|-------|
| 1 | Max continuous + fill to 100 | Farmer Carry | Easy swings (10L-10R x2-3) |
| 2 | Max continuous + fill to 100 | — | Plank & Shank or Farmer Carry |
| 3 | Max continuous + fill to 100 | — | Apex Hill Sprints (or KB HIC) |

Reduce swing frequency as barbell intensity increases. Combine sets week by week (e.g., 45-30-15-10 → 45-30-25 → 60-20-20 → eventually 100).

### Programming Recovery

#### Minimum Recovery Requirements
- 1 mandatory rest day per week (non-negotiable, no weights/sprints/LSS)
- 1 variable rest day per week (flexible based on how training is going)
- Rule of thumb for conditioning: 1-3 HIC/LSS sessions/week (counting BJJ/sport as conditioning)

#### 3-1-3-1 Recovery Approach
Recovery week every 4th week instead of every 6th:

| Week | Activity |
|------|----------|
| 1-3 | Black + Operator/Zulu |
| 4 | Recovery Week |
| 5-7 | Black + Operator/Zulu |
| 8 | Recovery Week |

Takes 8 weeks to complete a normal 6-week block, but allows higher total training volume. During recovery weeks: no barbells, no HIC. Limit to 2 easy workouts + sport/BJJ. Examples:

**Recovery Week Option A:**
- Sunday: Progressive Grip Circuit + mobility
- Monday: Off
- Tuesday: BJJ
- Wednesday: Off
- Thursday: Quarterdeck Core + mobility
- Friday: Off
- Saturday: BJJ

**Recovery Week Option B:**
- Sunday: Brig Rat (1-2 rounds) + mobility
- Monday: Off
- Tuesday: BJJ
- Wednesday: Off
- Thursday: Off
- Friday: 30-60 min LSS + mobility
- Saturday: BJJ

Every other block, take the second recovery week entirely free from serious training.

#### Recovery Routine
- **Sleep**: 7-8 hours minimum, non-negotiable. Higher quality/quantity workouts, fewer cravings, better dietary adherence.
- **Diet**: 1g protein/lb lean body weight, adequate carbs (especially during base building/conditioning emphasis), 1+ gallon water/day
- **Supplements**: Multivitamin, whey protein (2 scoops post-workout with berries + creatine), creatine monohydrate
- **Mobility**: 5-10 min dynamic warm-up before training, 15 min daily mobility work (bands, foam rolling, yoga), 10 min static stretching after conditioning/BJJ, extended 20-30 min sessions during recovery weeks

### Training Maxes

Use 90% of true 1RM as training max (or use 3RM/2RM directly). Reasons:
1. High-frequency programs require hitting numbers within 48-72 hours — need recovery room
2. Many training days happen in less-than-ideal recovery states (post-conditioning, post-BJJ, etc.)
3. Estimated 1RMs from rep maxes have built-in error margins
4. Advanced lifters need extra room due to higher absolute loads
5. Stress-free training — walk into gym knowing you can hit every number

Method: Put all 6 weeks in a spreadsheet. If any day looks questionable, lower the training max until every cell can be hit on your worst day.

### Testing Within the Block

Two methods (avoid burning recovery weeks on testing):

**Method 1 — Heavy Singles:** On last day of block, do 2 sets of doubles at 95%, then a single at 97.5-100% of training max. If crisp after heavy doubles, force progression.

**Method 2 — Rep Max:** On last day of block, do 2 sets of doubles at 95%, then one AMRAP set at 95% (stop before failure). Use formula to estimate new 1RM.

Only test when several blocks of solid progress suggest meaningful gains. Avoid gratuitous testing — it's costly for recovery and injury risk. Use modest forced progressions (5-10 lbs) between blocks as default.

### The Old Warhorse Template

Progressive training max peaking cycle for advanced lifters moving heavy weights. 4-block cycle:

| Block | Training Max | Lifting Volume | Conditioning | Test? |
|-------|-------------|----------------|--------------|-------|
| 1 | 80% of PR (conservative) | High (5-10 sets), OP I/A | Up to 3 HIC, push reps/intensity; E per continuation protocol | No |
| 2 | +2-5% from Block 1 | Moderate (4-8 sets), OP I/A | Up to 3 HIC/week; E per protocol | Your choice |
| 3 | +2-5% from Block 2 | Moderate (4-5 sets), Standard OP | Up to 2 HIC/week; E per protocol | Test within block |
| 4 | +2-7% from Block 3 (based on test) | Low (3-4 sets, minimal reps), Standard OP | 1 HIC (minimal) + up to 2 easy LSS (20-30 min) | 3-rep max at 100-107% t-max |

After Block 4: take days off, test properly, then restart at 80% training max or transition to base building/other goals.

### Back Break Templates

For giving the lower back periodic rest while maintaining lower body work.

**Squat Only Template** (1x/week squat, upper body at normal frequency):

| Week | Squat Protocol | Optional Down Sets |
|------|---------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Work to best 6 for the day | 1-3 sets of 6 at 80% of best 6 |
| 2 | Work to best 5 for the day | 1-3 sets of 5 at 85% of best 5 |
| 3 | Work to best 3 for the day | 1-3 sets of 3 at 90% of best 3 |

Two waves per block. "Best for the day" = best chill set with perfect form, no psyching up. Do squats on last lifting day of the week. Non-squat days: BP/WPU + Farmer Carry and KB Swings or Plank & Shank.

**Operator layout:**
- Day 1: BP/WPU + Farmer Carry + KB Swings/P&S
- Day 2: BP/WPU + Farmer Carry + KB Swings/P&S
- Day 3: BP/WPU/SQ to best set (+ optional down sets)

**Deadlift Only Template** (1x/week deadlift, upper body at normal frequency):

| Week | Deadlift Protocol | Optional Down Sets |
|------|------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | Work to best 5 for the day | 1-2 sets of 5 at 80% of best 5 |
| 2 | Work to best 4 for the day | 1-2 sets of 4 at 85% of best 4 |
| 3 | Work to best 3 for the day | 1-2 sets of 3 at 90% of best 3 |

Non-deadlift days: BP/WPU + squat-type accessories (goblet squats, double KB front squats, or pistols) at 3x5-10.

### Extended Summer Endurance Phase (Author's Approach)

10-12 weeks (vs standard 8). Typical week:
- Sunday: Off
- Monday: 6-9 mile LSS run
- Tuesday: BJJ
- Wednesday: Strength: circuits, calisthenics, climbing, etc.
- Thursday: 1600m swim
- Friday: Strength: circuits, calisthenics, climbing, etc.
- Saturday: 15-30 mile cycling or 10K Fun Run

LSS pace: 9 min/mile, observe talk test. Mix endurance modalities to avoid overuse (running → cycling → swimming rotation). Include monthly long Fun Runs with calisthenics intervals.

### Apex Hill Sprint Progression

Three keys to Apex progress:
1. **Build aerobic base** — sustaining 30+ min of continuous work requires aerobic endurance
2. **Master your kettlebell** — the swings should be the "easy part"
3. **Periodized approach** — treat like barbell training, stay at 60-85% effort most days

Mini-cycle:
- Week 1 — Distance emphasis: full hill for 60-90% of PR rounds
- Week 2 — Repetition emphasis: 1/2 distance for 75-90% perceived exertion

Run 3 mini-cycles alongside Operator/Zulu, then test at end of block. Frequency: 1 Apex session/week for aging athletes using heavier KBs.

### HIC Intensity for Aging Athletes
Always warm up: dynamic stretching → 800m jog → 2-3 building sprints at ~80%. First 1-2 reps of any sprint workout are diagnostic. Build pace throughout workout. If any twinge of trouble, moderate or shut down. Warm up is non-negotiable.

### Diet Strategies

**Food Journaling Approach:** Log everything for 2 weeks, analyze, cut obviously bad foods, repeat when progress stalls. Eventually builds intuition so logging isn't needed long-term.

**Routine Is King:** Find a set of foods and eating schedule that works, then follow it religiously. Preparing meals should be as routine as brushing teeth. Reduces food anxiety and decision fatigue.

**Sample Daily Meal Plan (training day, lunch workout):**
- 6:00 AM: 4 whole eggs, 2 slices organic toast, lemon juice, 1 quart water
- 9:00 AM: 1 cup plain Greek yogurt, spinach (sometimes fruit)
- 11:00 AM: Pre-workout: tablespoon chia seeds, 1 piece fruit
- 12:30 PM: Post-workout shake: 2 scoops whey, 5g creatine, 1 cup frozen berries
- 2:00 PM: 1 turkey burger, spinach, whole wheat crackers or brown rice
- 4:00 PM: 5 oz tuna, whole green pepper, handful almonds
- 6:00 PM: Family dinner (lean meat + lots of veggies)
- Friday: Cheat meal (whatever you want)

**Alcohol:** Moderate rigorously. Even 1-2 beers can cause lowered motivation, depressed mood, poor sleep lasting days. Significant correlation between near-abstinence and better performance/mood/body composition.

## Part 5: Persistence

### Discipline vs Emotional Intensity
Emotional intensity (being "fired up") is not sustainable long-term — it inevitably ebbs. Discipline is a consistent pattern of behavior followed independently of emotional state. Discipline is a habit, not a magical act of will. Training must become second nature like brushing teeth.

### Building Discipline (Aristotelian Approach)
"The virtues we get by first exercising them" (Aristotle). You don't have to like training at first — just go through the motions. Over time, the practice becomes habitual. Lower your short-term expectations: commit to just showing up and doing the minimum. Most days you'll end up doing more once you start.

### Guarding Against Doing Too Much
Four strategies:
1. **Self-knowledge** — know your personal temptations to overdo it
2. **Planned limits** — set maximum volume/intensity before each session
3. **Wise training partners** — find people who'll tell you to back off, not just push harder
4. **Get it out of your system** — occasional all-out sessions during recovery periods, with safety measures

### Competition
Schedule 2-3 competitions per year. Benefits: grounds training in reality, provides motivation, prevents narcissistic "gym hero" mentality, helps moderate daily training (don't risk injury before a race/tournament), keeps ego in check.
